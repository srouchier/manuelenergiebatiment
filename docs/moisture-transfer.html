Chapitre 7 Moisture transfer

Human activities in buildings generate water vapour, which is transported by air flow between rooms and between inside and outside. Moisture transfer also occurs within walls. It can generate condensation if the thermal properties of the envelope allow it, which may then degrade materials and lower the efficiency of insulation. The psychrometric chart, figure 7.1, is a diagram that shows the variables related to humidity. It allows evaluating the risks of condensation.

Figure 7.1: Psychrometric chart

The first important variable is the saturation vapour pressure, which is the maximum value that water vapour pressure can reach before condensation. It is a function of temperature only, here in degree Celsius: \[\begin{equation} p_\mathit{sat}(T) = \mathrm{exp} \left(23.5771-\frac{4042.9}{T+273.15-37.58}\right) \end{equation}\] The is the ratio between the vapour pressure and this saturation value: \[\begin{equation} \mathrm{RH} = \frac{p_v}{p_\mathit{sat}} \end{equation}\] RH ranges between 0\(\%\) (no humidity at all) and 100\(\%\)

The moisture content\(w\), sometimes called humidity ratio \(r\), is the quantity of water contained in air. In the following equation, its unit is [kg.kg\(^{-1}\)]: \(w\) is the mass of water contained in a certain mass of air. \[\begin{equation} w = 0.622 \frac{p_v}{p_\mathit{atm}-p_v} \end{equation}\] where \(p_\mathit{atm}\) is the atmospheric pressure. The moisture content can also be expressed in [kg.m\(^{-3}\)], simply by multiplying the above value with the density of air. A room with a volume \(V\) then contains a total mass of water: \[\begin{equation} M_{H_2O}=\rho_a \cdot V \cdot w \end{equation}\] This total mass of water in a room is the balance between incoming and outgoing water flow rates, and the humidity generated inside the room. The unit of these flows is [kg.s\(^{-1}\)]. \[\begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}M_{H_2O}}{\mathrm{d}t} = G_{H_2O,\mathrm{in}} - G_{H_2O, \mathrm{out}} + G_{H_2O,\mathrm{generated}} \end{equation}\] In particular, an air flow rate \(\dot{m}\) between two room that have moisture contents of \(w_1\) and \(w_2\) respectively, will carry a water flow rate of \(G=\dot{m}(w_1-w_2)\).

Figure 7.2: Psychrometrics

In air conditioning, air is described as a mixture of dry air and water vapour. Its properties can be read on the psychrometric chart, and are also related to each other by the following formula.

  • Dry bulb temperature \(T\), or just ``temperature’’.
  • The wet bulb temperature \(T_w\) is the temperature measured by a wet thermometer. It is the temperature that would be reached if the air was humidified until saturation at constant enthalpy.
  • The dew point temperature \(T_{dp}\) is the temperature at which condensation would start is the air was cooled at constant (absolute) humidity.
  • The relative humidity RH is the ratio between the vapor pressure of water \(p_\mathit{vap}\) and the saturation vapor pressure \(p_\mathit{sat}\), which is only a function of temperature, here in degree Celsius:

\[\begin{align} \mathrm{RH} & = \frac{p_\mathit{vap}}{p_\mathit{sat}} \\ p_\mathit{sat}(T) & = \mathrm{exp} \left(23.5771-\frac{4042.9}{T+273.15-37.58}\right) \end{align}\]

  • The moisture content \(w\), or absolute humidity, is the mass of water vapor per unit mass of dry air containing the vapor. Its unit is [kg\(_\mathit{vap}\)/kg\(_\mathit{da}\)].

\[\begin{equation} w = \frac{\rho_\mathit{vap}}{\rho_\mathit{da}} = \frac{r_\mathit{da}}{r_\mathit{vap}} \frac{p_\mathit{vap}}{p_\mathit{atm}-p_\mathit{vap}} \end{equation}\]

  • The specific enthalpy is the total energy of the dry air and water vapor, per unit mass of dry air [kJ/kg\(_{da}\)]. It is the addition of the sensible heat of dry air \(c_a \, T\) and the latent heat from the water vapor content:

\[\begin{equation} h = c_\mathit{da}\, T + w \, (l_v+c_\mathit{vap}\, T) \label{eq:enthalpy} \end{equation}\]

  • The specific volume \(v_s\) is the volume occupied by unit mass of dry air [m\(^3\)/kg\(_{da}\)]. It is the opposite of the dry air density \(\rho_\mathit{da}\).

\[\begin{equation} \rho_\mathit{da} = \frac{1}{v_s} = \frac{p_\mathit{atm}}{\left(w \, r_\mathit{vap}+r_\mathit{da}\right) T} \end{equation}\]

In the above definitions, the vap'' index stands for water vapour and theda’’ index stands for dry air. The constants that appear in the equations are summarised in this table:

Table 7.1: Variables and their units
Variable Dry air (da) Water vapour (vap)
Specific heat \(c_p\) [J.kg\(^{-1}\).K\(^{-1}\)] 1000 1850
Ideal gas constant \(r\) [J.kg\(^{-1}\).K\(^{-1}\)] 287 462
Latent heat of evaporation \(l_v\) [J.kg\(^{-1}\)] . \(2.5 \times 10^6\)

When mixing two air flows with mass of dry air \(m_{da,1}\) and \(m_{da,2}\), the equations for conservation of mass and energy allow obtaining the properties of the mixture \(m\) from the properties of each part \(1\) and \(2\): \[\begin{align} m_{da,m} & = m_{da,1} + m_{da,2} \\ m_{da,m} \, T_m & = m_{da,1} \, T_1 + m_{da,2} \, T_2 \\ m_{da,m} \, w_m & = m_{da,1} \, w_1 + m_{da,2} \, w_2 \\ m_{da,m} \, h_m & = m_{da,1} \, h_1 + m_{da,2} \, h_2 \end{align}\] The temperature, absolute humidity and enthalpy of the mixture are weighted averages of the properties of each component.

7.1 Moisture transfer in materials

Most building materials have the capacity to store and release moisture. There are simplified models to estimate this effect of moisture buffering on the variations of indoor humidity. We can also describe water transfer through walls, with a formulation that is similar to heat transfer: \[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial w}{\partial t} = -\mathrm{div} \left( g_\mathrm{vap} + g_\mathrm{liq} \right) \end{equation}\] where \(g_\mathrm{vap}\) and \(g_\mathrm{liq}\), in [kg.s\(^{-1}\).m\(^{-2}\)] are the flow rates per unit surface of vapour and liquid water, respectively. The moisture content \(w\) is expressed here in [kg.m\(^{-3}\)]. Liquid transfer is often neglected, unless there is significant rain leakage or capillary rise from the ground. The vapour flow rate follows Fick’s law, which is the equivalent of the heat conduction law: it is driven by a gradient of vapour pressure, and a permeability coefficient \(\delta_p\): \[\begin{equation} g_\mathrm{vap} = - \delta_\mathrm{vap} \nabla p_v \end{equation}\]

Figure 7.3: Steady state moisture transfer inside a wall

This means that in steady state, the distribution of vapour pressure inside a wall component with thickness \(e\) and permeability \(\delta_p\) is linear, as shown by Fig. 7.3. \[\begin{equation} g_\mathrm{vap} = \frac{\delta_\mathrm{vap}}{e}(p_{v1}-p_{v2}) \tag{7.1} \end{equation}\] We can therefore define the moisture transfer resistance of a wall component as \(R=e/\delta_\mathrm{vap}\). The moisture exchange between the surface of a wall and the ambient air can also be formulated in the same way as heat transfer, with a surface moisture transfer coefficient \(h_m\): \[\begin{equation} g_\mathrm{vap} = h_m (p_{v,\mathrm{surf}}-p_{v,\mathrm{air}}) \tag{7.2} \end{equation}\]

Equations (7.1) and (7.2) can be used to calculate the distribution of vapour pressure across a multi-layered wall, like we would do with temperatures. This allows identifying eventual condensation spots.
