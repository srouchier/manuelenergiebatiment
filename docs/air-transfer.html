Chapitre 6 Air transfer

6.1 Principles

The calculation of air flow rates in buildings due to natural ventilation are based on two simplified equations that relate the air pressure to other environmental variables: Bernoulli’s law and the ideal gas law.

Bernoulli’s law relates the pressure \(P\) between several points along a streamline, with their speed \(U\), density \(\rho\) and elevation \(z\). \[\begin{equation} \frac{1}{2}\, \rho \, U^2+P+\rho_a \, g \, z = \mathrm{constant} \tag{6.1} \end{equation}\]

The ideal gas law relates pressure \(P\), density \(\rho\) and temperature \(T\). \[\begin{equation} P = \rho \, R_a \, T \tag{6.2} \end{equation}\] This equation can be summarized to approximately relate the air density directly to its temperature in Kelvin: \[\begin{equation} \rho \approx \frac{353}{T} \tag{6.3} \end{equation}\]

The two main forces that drive natural ventilation across buildings are the wind and the stack effect.

The wind brings an increase or decrease of air pressure at the outer surfaces of a building. This pressure variation depends on an empirical wind pressure coefficient \(C_p\) (see Fig. 6.1):

Figure 6.1: Wind pressure coefficients

\[\begin{equation} \Delta P = \frac{1}{2} \, \rho \, C_p \, U_\mathit{wind}^2 \tag{6.4} \end{equation}\]

The stack effect is the consequence of variations of air density with the temperature. If the indoor and outdoor temperatures are different, then so are the air densities. The difference of pressure between inside and outside depends on the elevation \(z\) and on the pressure difference at ground level: \[\begin{equation} \Delta P(z) = \Delta P(0) - g \cdot z \cdot \rho_e \cdot \frac{T_i-T_e}{T_i} \tag{6.5} \end{equation}\] When two openings are separated by a height \(H\), the global pressure difference between them reads: \[\begin{equation} \Delta P = g \cdot H \cdot (\rho_e-\rho_i) = g \cdot H \cdot \rho_e \cdot \frac{T_i-T_e}{T_i} \tag{6.6} \end{equation}\]

Figure 6.2: Stack effect

The stack effect is caused by this relation between density and temperature. The two air volumes shown here have temperatures notes \[T_e\] and \[T_i\]. The pressure difference between them is a function of the coordinate \[z\]:

\[ P_e(z) - P_i(z) = \left( P_e(0)-\rho_e \, g \, z\right) - \left( P_i(0)-\rho_i \, g \, z \right) = P_e(0)-P_i(0)-\rho_e \, g \, z \, \frac{T_i-T_e}{T_i}\]

The neutral plane is the coordinate \[z_n\] at which this pressure difference is equal to zero:

\[z_n = \dfrac{P_e(0)-P_i(0)}{g(\rho_i-\rho_e)} \]

6.2 Air flow network

The air flow network is the most simple and most common method to formulate air flow rates from the pressure gradients formulated above. It represents each zone of the building with a uniform value of all state variables (pressure, temperature, density…).

A small opening with an area \[S\] and a discharge coefficient \[C_d\] separate two rooms at the pressures \[P_1\] and \[P_2\]. The air flow rate \[Q_v\] [m\[^3\]/s] through the opening can be estimated by:

\[ P_1 - P_2 = \frac{\rho Q_v^2}{2S^2C_d^2} \]

The air flow rate \(Q\) through a large opening between two zones, or between the inside and outside, can be formulated as: \[\begin{equation} Q = A^* \sqrt{\frac{2\cdot|\Delta P|}{\rho}} \tag{6.7} \end{equation}\] where \(A^*\) is the effective area of the opening. It depends on the real area of the opening and a discharge coefficient \(C_d\): \[\begin{equation} A^*=A\cdot C_d \tag{6.8} \end{equation}\] The discharge coefficient can be close to 1 for a large opening in a laminar flow, but is set below 1 most of the time. If air is flowing across a room through two opposite openings, then the total effective area can be formulated from the areas and discharge coefficients of the inlet and outlet openings: \[\begin{equation} A^* = \frac{1}{\sqrt{ \left(\frac{1}{C_{d1}\cdot A_1}\right)^2 +\left(\frac{1}{C_{d2}\cdot A_2}\right)^2 }} \tag{6.9} \end{equation}\] In case of air infiltration through small cracks, equation (6.7) has a slightly different form, with an exponent \(n\) close to \(1/2\): \[\begin{equation} Q = K \cdot \left|\Delta P\right|^n \tag{6.10} \end{equation}\]

The resulting air flow rate can be formulated from the environmental variables by including the contributions of wind and stack effect into equation (6.7). If air flows through two openings, separated by an elevation \(H\), the flow induced by the stack effect can be added or opposed to the flow induced by wind. After checking which direction each of these two forces will take, the following equation can be used to either add or substract them: \[\begin{equation} Q=A^* \left( \sqrt{\left|\Delta C_p\right|\cdot U_\mathit{wind}^2 } \pm \sqrt{2\cdot g\cdot H \left(\frac{|\Delta T|}{T_\mathit{mean}}\right) } \right) \tag{6.11} \end{equation}\] where \(T_\mathit{mean}\) is the average temperature between inside and outside, in Kelvin.

6.3 Tutorials

6.3.1 Tutorial 1: air flow through a door

A door of height \[H=2\]m and width \[w=1\]m separates two rooms at temperatures \[T_1=15°C\] and \[T_2=21°C\].

Suppose (without proving it) that the air velocity at the interface is a function of the coordinate \[z\]:

\[ V(z) = \sqrt{2\, \frac{P_1(z)-P_2(z)}{\rho}} \]

where \[\rho\] is the mass density on the side from which air flows (which depends on whether the coordinate is above or below the neutral plane).

Integrate \[V(z)\] over the height \[H\] to find the total mass flow rate of air \[Q_m\] [kg/s] through the door. Suppose the door is an infinite stack of small openings with a discharge coefficient \[C_d=0.4212\].

Solution

Below the neutral plane, air flows from room 1 to room 2 with a total rate of:

\[Q_{m,12} = C_d \int_0^{z_n} \rho_1 \, V(z) \, w \, \mathrm{d}z \]

Above the neutral plane, air flows from room 2 to room 1 with a total rate of:

\[Q_{m,21} = C_d \int_{z_n}^{H} \rho_2 \, V(z) \, w \, \mathrm{d}z \]

The net air flow rate from 1 to 2 is \[Q_{m,12}-Q_{m,21}\]

6.3.3 Tutorial 3: solar chimney and evaporative cooling

We are studying a solar chimney shown on Fig. 6.4. The air is heated inside the chimney, which creates a stack effect that drives it upwards. The pressure drop creates an air flow into the house, which first goes through a ground-coupled heat exchanger. The target is to calculate the indoor temperature as a consequence of this cold air flow.

Figure 6.4: Solar chimney

Inside the chimney, air circulates between a glass collector and a concrete slab. In order to solve the problem, we need at least three equations:

  1. Write the heat balance of the main room of the house (not the chimney) as function of its global heat transfer coefficient \(H=100\) W/K and incoming air flow rate \(\dot{m}\).
  2. Formulate the mass flow rate as function of the air temperatures at the inlet \(T_\mathrm{in}=13\)°C and at the outlet of the chimney \(T_\mathrm{out}\), and the difference of elevation \(z\) between them.
  3. Write the heat balance inside the chimney in order to estimate the maximum air temperature that can be reached inside it.

The thermal properties of the chimney and convective heat transfer coefficients can be taken from previous exercises.
