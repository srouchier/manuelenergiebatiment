5.1 Heat capacity

All the above equations can be solved in steady-state to calculate the value of each temperature in the discretization shown on Fig. ??. In a non-steady state, temperatures can vary: the evolution of each temperature is caused by an inbalance between the incoming and outgoing heat flux: \[\begin{equation} C \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \Phi_\mathit{in} - \Phi_\mathit{out} \tag{5.1} \end{equation}\] where \(C\) (J.K\(^{-1}\)) is the heat capacitance assigned to this point.

One-dimensional transient heat transfer through a wall of density \(\rho\), heat capacity \(c_p\) and conductivity \(\lambda\), reads: \[\begin{equation} \rho \, c_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \lambda \frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2} \tag{5.2} \end{equation}\]

Figure 5.1: Heat capacity of a wall

Let us suppose that the wall is discretised into three temperature points separated by two elements of thickness \(\Delta x/2\), as shown on Fig. 5.1. Then the temperature inside the wall can be expressed as function of the two boundary temperatures, a thermal capacitance and two thermal resistances: \[\begin{equation} \left(\rho \, \Delta x \, c_p \right) \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \frac{T_1 - T}{\Delta x / 2\lambda} + \frac{T_2 - T}{\Delta x / 2\lambda} \tag{5.3} \end{equation}\]

A thermal capacitance can be assigned to the temperature point of an air volume in order to calculate its evolution. As an approximation, some points of the discretisation may not have a capacitance, if we consider that they are quick to reach a steady balance with their surroundings. This is the case for light materials such as windows.

5.3 Tutorial: thermal inertia

Figure 5.2: Tutorial: thermal inertia

We represent a building with a single thermal resistance \(R\) (from the global heat transfer coefficient) and a single heat capacitance \(C\): see Fig. \(\ref{fig:inertia1}\). A heating power \(\Phi\) is imposed on the indoor temperature. The evolution of the indoor temperature is thus driven by the differential equation: \[\begin{equation} C \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{R} \left(T_e-T_i\right) + \Phi \tag{5.4} \end{equation}\]

5.3.1 Question 1: winter conditions

We suppose that the building is initially at the same temperature \(T_e\) as the outside. The heating is then turned on at a constant power \(\Phi\).

  1. Solve equation \(\ref{eq:inertia1}\) to show that the evolution of the indoor temperature is: \[\begin{equation} T_i = T_e + R \, \Phi \left(1-\mathrm{exp}\left(-\frac{t}{RC}\right)\right) \tag{5.5} \end{equation}\]
  2. Draw the evolution of \(T_i\). How does this graph change with different values of \(R\) and \(C\)?

5.3.2 Question 2: summer conditions

Figure 5.3: Summer temperature

We now suppose that the outdoor temperature is a sine wave (see Fig. 5.3). There is no heating: \(\Phi=0\). The outdoor temperature varies between 10°C and 30°C, with a period of 24h. \[\begin{align} T_e & = 20 + 10 \, \mathrm{sin}(\omega \, t) \tag{5.6} \\ \omega & = \frac{2 \pi}{24 \times 3600} \tag{5.7} \end{align}\] We can show that the complex impedance of the building is: \[\begin{equation} Z(j\omega) = \frac{T_i}{T_e} = \frac{1}{1+jRC\omega} \tag{5.8} \end{equation}\] Draw the evolution of \(T_i\). How does this graph change with different values of \(R\) and \(C\)?
